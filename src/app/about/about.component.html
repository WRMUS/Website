About The "What Really Makes Us Safe?" Project
Melanie Brazzell
Research process
The “What Really Makes Us Safe?” Project began as an informal exchange between transformative justice practitioners responding to sexual and partner violence. Over the years, it grew into an interview project that became academic research, the results of which are presented here on this website as a movement resource and archive. As initiator of of the project, I also brought back its lessons to my home context in Berlin, Germany, resulting in public events and a toolkit.

I want to narrate a bit of the project’s evolution. Our fledgling transformative justice collective in Berlin was inspired by organizing in the U.S. but geographically far from the mothership. We often turned to U.S.-based activists for guidance, sharing our surprises and struggles. Dialoguing with those who had helped create this movement became a way to both give and take account of one another.

That informal exchange expanded into a participatory research project in 2013 as the foundations of my Master’s thesis. The research explored the theoretical underpinnings of community accountability and transformative justice, like: What does it mean to be accountable, actually? And who, or what, is a community?

Participatory action research
Participatory action research is an approach that understands all participants in a research process to be co-creators of the project’s goals, methods, and outcomes.

In my interviews with transformative justice practitioners, our dialogue offered an opportunity to think through questions emerging out of our respective practices. Interview partners shared organizational materials, connected me with other interviewees, and worked with me to ensure their voices were properly documented.

Fuller participation in the project was limited by capacity and distance (interviewees were scattered across North America). Unable to all sit at a table, the project took the shape of a net weaving from one node of organizing to another, as questions and ideas developed in one conversation were carried to the next. What is documented here is a map of that process, putting those conversations into dialogue with one another directly.

Who was involved?

All in all, 16 activist-thinkers gave interviews. You can read their full biographies here. I was the primary interviewer, and several early interviews were done by comrades from the Transformative Justice Kollektiv Berlin.

What were the results?

Many interviewees described the need to share and archive transformative justice work, as our practices are nascent and experimental and rarely documented. As a result, I built this site to amplify oral histories from the movement and share knowledge with other practitioners. It also introduces a broader audience to the practices and principles of transformative justice. I filled in any gaps on the site left by my interviews with additional quotes from the writing of other transformative justice practitioners and thinkers not directly involved in this project, in the hope of giving the fullest possible picture of the movement.

Academic publications resulting from this project can be found here.

About The Website
Collective Rather Than SIngle-Story Approach

Many accounts of transformative justice work offer stories or models for a single community-based process, following the narrative arcs of ecstatic ups and catastrophic downs and the interactions between various players as they move through one process.

This website approaches transformative justice differently. Rather than looking at full stories, the website gathers several voices and hears what they each have to say about the same general question: What is a community after all? How do we get people to change? Where is the movement headed?

As we move away from the vision of a single, holistic process as the main way to do transformative justice work, I hope this equips us with more heterogenous and eclectic approaches.

About the website
Vertical, not horizontal approach
Many accounts of CA-TJ work currently out there offer narratives or models for a single community-based process, following narrative arcs of ups and downs and the interactions between various players as they move through a process. This website approaches CA-TJ work differently: by cutting it vertically rather than horizontally. Rather than looking at full stories, the website gathers several voices and hears what they each have to say about the same general question: What is a community after all? How do we get people to change? Where is the movement headed?
How to navigate the site
To access these questions, you can navigate through various 'chapters':

  • ‘Collaborators’ offers biographies of the activists interviewed, as well as their personal and organizational stories.
  • The chapter ‘Movement’ puts the movement in its larger historical and social context, including a section on the Principles behind the work.
  • ‘Stories’ focuses on the interviewees’ praxis: Support for survivors, Accountability for those who cause harm, and engagement with the broader Community.
