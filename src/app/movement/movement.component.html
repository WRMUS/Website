Transformative justice imagines a community-based response to sexual and partner violence that humanizes, rather than criminalizes, people who cause harm. When possible, transformative justice can engage them in a process to take accountability for their actions –to repair existing harm and transform underlying conditions and behaviors to prevent future harm. What can those processes look like?
From carceral to transformative approaches
While the other sections of this site focus mainly on practice, this section offers a more theoretical view on community accountability. Drawing on both interview excerpts and other resources by movement activist-thinkers, I propose several main principles that unite various community accountability & transformative justice responses to violence. Each of the following 5 principles compares a carceral approach to violence with a transformative one. This chart gives a brief summary:
Carceral Approach Transformative Approach
Ethos Individual “bad apples” & individual solutions Collective collective problem & community solutions
People Causing Violence Criminalization Good vs. evil, demonize or minimize Humanization everyone can cause or experience harm
Concept of Responsibility Blame & Punishment ‘just deserts’ ‘eye for an eye’ debt Accountability personal & structural prevention, intervention, reparation, transformation
People Experiencing Violence Security paternalistic protection Safety tools for healing & self determination
Response to Violence Containment & Disposal "dangerous" people contained or removed Connection & Community building relationships and collective power
